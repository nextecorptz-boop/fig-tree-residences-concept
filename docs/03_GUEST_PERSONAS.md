# Fig Tree Residences — Guest Personas
### Phase 2: Experience Strategy — v1.0

Six personas, grounded in what Discovery actually established about the property's location and guest base rather than generic hospitality archetypes: Masaki is a diplomatic, NGO, and international-business-dense peninsula, within one to two kilometres of two international schools, an Aga Khan clinic, and a yacht club, and the property's own recent content (its one 2025 blog post) is explicitly written for the long-stay guest. Each persona below defines goals, needs, pain points, luxury expectations, and website expectations, and each should be treated as a lens for later decisions in `04_GUEST_JOURNEY.md` onward, not as a marketing segment to write ad copy for.

## 1. The Business Traveler

**Goals:** Get in, get productive, get out, with minimal personal overhead spent on logistics.
**Needs:** Reliable WiFi and a real desk (both already promised in the brochure), a straightforward way to see rates and book without a phone call, proximity to the city's commercial districts, and a quiet environment to recover in after long days.
**Pain Points:** Corporate hotel rooms that feel identical to every other airport hotel in the world; opaque or phone-only booking that costs time they don't have; uncertainty about whether a small property can support a business trip's practical needs.
**Luxury Expectations:** Luxury, for this guest, is time saved and friction removed — a seamless, fast, trustworthy digital booking experience is itself the luxury, not marble floors.
**Website Expectations:** Visible rates, a fast and obvious booking path, clear confirmation of WiFi/desk/workspace amenities, and enough visual proof of quality to book without needing a phone call to reassure them.

## 2. Embassy Staff

**Goals:** Secure, discreet, dependable accommodation for postings or extended visits, often arranged in part through an institutional process rather than a purely personal one.
**Needs:** Demonstrable security (coded access, monitored perimeter — already a Fig Tree strength per the brochure's own longest amenity list), privacy from public view, proximity to embassies and diplomatic social infrastructure already clustered around Masaki, and consistency they can vouch for to colleagues.
**Pain Points:** Properties that market "security" as a checklist rather than something felt on arrival; uncertainty about who else is on the premises; a lack of institutional-grade clarity (rates, policies, invoicing) that a posting officer can present upward.
**Luxury Expectations:** Discretion and reliability outrank visible opulence; this guest's idea of luxury is a property that never becomes a problem they have to manage.
**Website Expectations:** Clear, confident security and privacy messaging (not defensive-sounding), straightforward rate and policy information suitable for institutional booking, and a tone that reads as established and dependable rather than trendy.

## 3. The NGO Professional

**Goals:** A stable base for field-adjacent work in Dar es Salaam, frequently on irregular schedules, often on a per-diem or organisational budget with real cost sensitivity.
**Needs:** A genuine kitchenette (already standard in every unit) to control food costs and schedule, flexible-feeling booking for uncertain trip lengths, a quiet place to decompress after demanding field or coordination work, and straightforward, transparent pricing to reconcile against an expense report.
**Pain Points:** Hotels that assume every guest is on holiday or on an expense account with no ceiling; noisy, high-traffic properties that offer no real recovery space; unclear or hidden pricing that complicates reimbursement.
**Luxury Expectations:** For this guest, luxury is quiet dignity on a real budget — a residence that treats a modestly priced long stay with the same care as an expensive one, not a discount experience.

**Website Expectations:** Transparent, easy-to-find long-stay rates, plain language about the kitchenette and self-catering amenities that matter to this guest's actual daily life, and a tone that respects their work rather than selling them a vacation.

## 4. The Long-Stay Resident

**Goals:** Somewhere that functions as a genuine home for weeks or months — the exact guest the property's own 2025 blog post ("Benefits of Long-Term Stays in Serviced Apartments") was already written for.
**Needs:** Laundry facilities, a full kitchen, a sense of neighbourhood and routine (garden, pool, rooftop as places to actually live in, not just photograph), and a relationship with staff that develops over time rather than resetting every visit.
**Pain Points:** Serviced apartments that feel transactional and impersonal despite the "home" language in their marketing; a lack of any content or story that acknowledges what daily life there is actually like past the first week.
**Luxury Expectations:** Luxury here is belonging — the small, cumulative comforts of being recognised by name, having a preferred table on the rooftop, and having a garden that starts to feel like their own.
**Website Expectations:** Real depth on long-stay life (garden, routine, community, not just room specs), a rates page that treats extended stays as a first-class option rather than an asterisk, and storytelling that goes beyond a single night's experience.

## 5. The Family

**Goals:** A safe, comfortable base for a stay that includes children — whether visiting relatives working locally, relocating, or travelling together — within reach of the international schools and clinic already clustered nearby.
**Needs:** Space (the sofa-bed configuration in every unit already supports this), a secure, enclosed outdoor area for children (the extensive gardens and pool), self-catering flexibility for children's meals and schedules, and visible, genuine safety.
**Pain Points:** Hotels that treat children as an inconvenience or an add-on charge; a lack of any garden, outdoor, or child-safe imagery to reassure a parent booking from abroad; ambiguity about whether the pool and grounds are actually supervised or secure.
**Luxury Expectations:** Luxury, for this guest, is the ability to relax while their children are somewhere genuinely safe — space and calm outrank amenity lists.
**Website Expectations:** Photography and copy that shows the gardens and pool as real, livable family space, plain reassurance about safety and security, and clarity about room configuration for a family group.

## 6. The Returning Guest

**Goals:** To come back to something they already trust, with as little re-decision-making as possible.
**Needs:** Recognition (of history, of preference, of relationship), continuity (the same calm garden, the same rooftop sunset, the same staff), and a fast, frictionless way to book again without relitigating the choice from scratch.
**Pain Points:** Being treated as a first-time visitor every time; a website experience built entirely around persuading a stranger, with nothing built for someone who has already decided.
**Luxury Expectations:** For this guest, luxury is being known — the opposite of being sold to. The best luxury signal Fig Tree can send this persona is evidence that it remembers them.
**Website Expectations:** A fast, low-friction return-booking path (ideally a recognisable "welcome back" moment), continuity of imagery and story from their last visit, and content (reviews, staff stories, garden seasons) that rewards someone who already knows the property, not just content aimed at first-time persuasion.

## 7. What All Six Have in Common

Every persona above converges on the same three things Discovery already identified as the brand's own load-bearing pillars: privacy, security, and calm. The differences between personas are about *why* each of those three matters to them — cost-conscious dignity for the NGO professional, institutional dependability for embassy staff, child safety for the family — not about needing a fundamentally different property or story. This is a strong signal that the coming journey, architecture, and copy work should differentiate framing and emphasis by persona, not build six separate experiences.
