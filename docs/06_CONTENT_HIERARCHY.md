# Fig Tree Residences — Content Hierarchy
### Phase 2: Experience Strategy — v1.0

This document ranks what the experience needs to say, in order of importance, and explains why. It defines priority, not layout — no page structure, wireframe, or visual weight is specified here; that belongs to `07_INFORMATION_ARCHITECTURE.md` and later design phases.

## 1. The Principle Behind the Ranking

Content earns a high rank here for one of two reasons: either it is a fact Discovery confirmed is genuinely rare and differentiating (location, security depth, the fig-tree story), or it is a piece of information whose absence actively breaks trust (rates, real room detail). Content that is merely pleasant but replaceable — generic amenity photography, decorative copy — ranks lower, regardless of how much space it might visually occupy.

## 2. Tier One: Load-Bearing Content

This is content the experience cannot succeed without, because its absence was identified in Discovery as actively damaging trust or leaving the property's best assets invisible.

- **Real, visible rates**, or a clear, deliberately worded "rates on request" framing if that is the intended model. Nothing else in this hierarchy matters if a guest cannot get a straight answer to "what does this cost."
- **The location story, told in full** — the 200-metre distance to the Yacht Club, the walk to the Slipway, the proximity to international schools and clinics — because Discovery found this to be the single most specific, most differentiating content asset in the entire project, currently trapped in a brochure.
- **Genuine room differentiation** — at minimum, distinct treatment for the older-style rooms and the newer arched-headboard rooms, with square-metreage and configuration facts already available from the brochure.
- **A working, trustworthy booking path**, free of the triplicated payment icons and the pre-filled "success" message currently found on the live contact form.

## 3. Tier Two: Trust-Building Content

This is content that does not break the experience if delayed, but that directly builds the "I never want to leave" feeling once Tier One is solid.

- **The brand story** — the fig-tree metaphor, the 2018 opening, the Cicero line — none of which currently appears on the live site at all.
- **Security, reframed as reassurance rather than a checklist** — the nine-point security list in the brochure is real content; it needs translation into feeling, not more bullets.
- **The gardens as a destination**, not a backdrop — dedicated space for the living wall, mature trees, and outdoor seating that already photograph well.
- **Reviews and third-party validation**, brought onto the property's own site rather than left solely on OTA pages the property does not control the framing of.
- **Staff and human presence** — currently represented by exactly one photograph across the entire asset library, against an explicit written promise of "a happy, bright and friendly collection of professionals."

## 4. Tier Three: Depth Content

This is content that rewards a guest who is already convinced and wants more — valuable, but not something a first-time visitor needs to see before deciding.

- **Long-stay life detail** (laundry, kitchen routines, garden seasons) for the Long-Stay Resident and returning-guest audiences identified in `03_GUEST_PERSONAS.md`.
- **A living blog or journal**, picking up the thread the property's own October 2025 post already started, rather than leaving it as an isolated one-off after a seven-year gap.
- **A departure/return moment** — content that exists specifically for the end of a stay, an entirely new category the current site has no equivalent of at all.

## 5. What Should Not Be Prioritised

Generic hospitality-marketing language already found across the current site ("comfort and style in soft relaxing ocean colours") ranks intentionally low in this hierarchy — it is the kind of content any hotel could say about itself, and Discovery's brand analysis found the property already has far more specific, ownable material available. Decorative stock-style copy should be replaced by specific truth wherever the two compete for the same space.

## 6. How This Hierarchy Should Be Used

Any future page, section, or content decision should be checked against this ranking before it is checked against how it looks. A visually striking section built from Tier Three content, while Tier One content (rates, room differentiation) is still missing, is a hierarchy violation regardless of how well it is designed.
