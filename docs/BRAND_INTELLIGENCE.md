# Fig Tree Residences — Brand Intelligence
### Phase 1A: Discovery & Asset Intelligence

This document interprets the visual identity, voice, and positioning signals found across the brochure, the physical property, and the live website. It draws conclusions from what already exists; it does not propose new brand direction.

## 1. The Name and the Mark

"Fig Tree Residences" is built around a literal, illustrated fig tree: a rounded, leafy canopy dotted with orange fruit, sitting on a visible trunk with an equally visible root system illustrated below the ground line. The roots are not a minor detail — they are given as much visual weight as the canopy in the mark, which reads deliberately as a growth-and-groundedness metaphor rather than a decorative flourish. The wordmark splits the name across the trunk ("FIG" to the left, "TREE" to the right) with "RESIDENCES" in a smaller, different colour beneath — a construction that only fully works when the illustration and the wordmark are locked together as a single unit, which is a constraint worth respecting in any future logo lockup or simplification.

The mark currently exists only as a photograph of a painted sign and as a small raster reproduction on the brochure; there is no vector master anywhere in the project. This means the logo, as an asset, is not yet in a state where it can be resized, recoloured, or reproduced cleanly, and re-drawing or professional vectorisation is a near-term dependency for almost any other brand deliverable.

## 2. Colour

Two colour families recur consistently enough, across enough independent sources, to be treated as the property's real, already-established palette rather than an accident of individual photographs:

- **Deep teal / turquoise-green** — used for the tree trunk and "FIG TREE" wordmark on the logo, and independently used as the actual paint colour of the building's exterior walls, the rooftop bar structure, and the physical signboard background. This colour appears far too consistently, across too many unrelated sources (the sign, the building, the brochure's own panel backgrounds), to be coincidental — it is functionally already the brand's primary colour.
- **Terracotta / coral-orange** — used for the fig fruit on the logo and the "RESIDENCES" wordmark, and echoed independently in the property's stone garden walls and low retaining walls, which are built from a warm terracotta-toned rock.
- **Cream / off-white** — the neutral ground colour used behind the logo on the brochure cover and consistent with the building's interior wall colour in most photographs (allowing for the colour-cast issue documented in `PHOTO_AUDIT.md`, which artificially shifts this cream toward green in several images).

A recurring soft-blue/navy accent also appears independently across multiple guest rooms (bed runners, cushions, curtains) and in a nautical sailboat painting that recurs in at least three separate photographs — likely a deliberate nod to the Yacht Club location rather than incidental styling.

## 3. Typography and Voice

No font specimen or style guide exists, so typeface identification here is observational rather than exact. The logo wordmark uses a clean, geometric sans/serif hybrid in solid caps; brochure body copy uses a simple sans-serif for lists and a script/handwriting-style face for the cover quotation, which is a common but slightly inconsistent pairing — two very different typographic registers (structured caps versus a loose script) doing different jobs without a documented system tying them together.

The written voice across the brochure and website is warm, hospitable, and slightly informal, occasionally to the point of looseness: phrases like "you may never see another guest," "Bar b que," and "sun-downer" read as genuinely written by someone at the property rather than by a marketing agency, which is an asset in terms of authenticity but produces inconsistent capitalisation, spelling ("Accomodation," "aray"), and punctuation across published pages. The Cicero quotation on the brochure cover — "The pursuit, even of the best things, ought to be calm and tranquil" — is the single most deliberate piece of brand voice in the entire project and is currently used nowhere on the live website; it is a strong, differentiated line that is being wasted.

## 4. Positioning Signals — and a Real Inconsistency

The brochure and website both describe the product in residence/apartment language: "boutique residence," "serviced apartments," "apartments... fully stocked with kitchen and balcony," explicit square-metreage, and a dedicated recent blog post about the benefits of long-term stays. This is a coherent, differentiated positioning against a conventional hotel.

Every third-party channel that lists the property, however — TripAdvisor, Booking.com, Hotels.com, Expedia — categorises and lists it as a **hotel**, with room counts and amenity language that flattens the apartment-living differentiation the brand itself is trying to build. A guest arriving via an OTA search will form a "hotel" expectation that the property's own brochure and long-stay blog content are actively trying to move away from. This is not a wording problem that a redesign alone will fix — it is a channel-strategy question (how the property is categorised and described on every OTA listing) that sits outside the scope of a website rebuild but directly undermines it if left unaddressed.

## 5. What the Property Says About Itself, Unprompted

Three claims recur across the brochure and site without being asked for, and are worth treating as the brand's own self-image rather than assumptions this audit is introducing: privacy and exclusivity ("our premises are reserved exclusively for our guests," "you may never see another guest"), security (the only amenity list to receive nine separate bullet points is the security list, more than any other category), and calm ("tranquillity," "calm and tranquil," "quiet relaxation"). Any redesign should treat privacy, security, and calm as the three load-bearing pillars of the existing brand rather than inventing new ones, since the property has independently repeated all three across every piece of collateral this audit reviewed.

## 6. Unresolved Brand Questions

Two framed portraits of Tanzanian public figures hang in the reception/office area in more than one photograph, competing for wall space with the branded environment; whether these should remain, be relocated, or be replaced as part of a redesign is a client decision, not a design assumption. Similarly, the reception staff uniform (seen in the one available staff photograph) is Fig Tree–branded in teal — a genuine, existing uniform/livery system that could anchor future staff photography, but its full specification (is there a full uniform guide, or was this one polo shirt) is unknown.
