# Fig Tree Residences — Luxury Opportunities
### Phase 1A: Discovery & Asset Intelligence

This document synthesises every gap and strength identified across the preceding six reports into a single prioritised list of opportunities. It describes *what* the gap or opportunity is and *why* it matters; it deliberately stops short of prescribing visual design, layout, or code, which belong to a later phase.

## 1. Fix the Things That Actively Undermine Trust

A prospective guest who can find no room rates on a page literally titled "Rooms & Rates," who sees a contact form that claims to have already succeeded before it has been touched, and who notices the same payment icon rendered three times, will reasonably wonder what else on the site has not been finished. These are not aesthetic problems; they are trust problems, and in a luxury or boutique context — where the entire value proposition rests on an impression of care and attention to detail — they carry disproportionate weight. Publishing real rates (or a clear, deliberate "rates on request" framing if that is the intended model), fixing the contact-form default state, correcting the mislabeled social icons, and removing the duplicate payment buttons are the highest-leverage, lowest-difficulty fixes available.

## 2. Resolve the Domain Story

The brochure trains every guest who picks it up to look for figtree.co.tz; the actual working website lives at figtreeresidences.com. Before any redesign work begins, someone needs to decide — and confirm ownership and control of both domains — which address is permanent. A guest typing the brochure's own printed address into a browser should land somewhere coherent, not nowhere, or somewhere different from what a search engine returns.

## 3. Turn the Existing Colour System Into a Real Asset

The teal-and-terracotta palette already exists independently in three places — the logo, the painted building, and the garden stonework — which is a genuine, already-validated brand system that most properties would have to invent from scratch. The opportunity is not to choose new colours; it is to formalise the colours that already exist (exact values, usage rules, a proper vector logo redraw) so that every future touchpoint, digital or physical, draws from the same system instead of approximating it photograph by photograph.

## 4. Solve the Colour-Cast Problem Before It Reaches a Redesign

The newest, best-composed photography in the entire library — the arched palm-headboard bedrooms — is also the photography most visibly damaged by an uncorrected colour cast. Correcting or reshooting this specific set is a comparatively small, contained piece of work with an outsized payoff, because it unlocks images that are otherwise already close to luxury-ready.

## 5. Build a Real Room-Type Photography Set

With roughly 24 units and a "Rooms & Rates" page that shows no differentiation between them, there is a clear opportunity to photograph and name distinct room experiences (for example, the older-style room versus the newer arched-headboard studio) so a guest can actually see what they are booking rather than one generic representative image standing in for the whole property.

## 6. Give the Brand a Voice on Its Own Website

The single most distinctive piece of brand voice in the entire project — the Cicero quotation on the brochure cover — currently appears nowhere on the live site. Similarly, the property's own origin story (soft launch May 2018, full opening August 2018) exists only in a seven-year-old blog post and is absent from the About page that should be telling it. There is a ready-made, differentiated narrative sitting unused.

## 7. Make the Human Element Visible

Out of thirty-seven images, exactly one shows a person — a smiling staff member in a branded uniform. The brochure and About page both explicitly promise a "happy, bright and friendly collection of professionals," but the photography does not back that promise up. A boutique, service-led positioning is exactly the context where authentic staff and guest-experience photography earns its keep.

## 8. Reconcile How the Property Is Sold Everywhere Outside Its Own Site

Every OTA listing categorises Fig Tree as a hotel; every piece of the property's own writing positions it as a serviced-apartment, long-stay-friendly residence. A guest's first impression is increasingly likely to form on an OTA, not on figtreeresidences.com, so this inconsistency will quietly undercut a redesigned site's positioning unless it is addressed as part of the same project, even though it is technically a channel-management issue rather than a website issue.

## 9. Add the Channels a Tanzanian Guest Actually Expects

There is no WhatsApp contact anywhere on the site, despite WhatsApp being a dominant enquiry and booking channel in this market, and no Instagram presence despite the property's location-and-lifestyle story being a natural fit for that platform. Both are comparatively low-effort additions with a plausible, direct impact on enquiry volume.

## 10. Correct the Small Things That Are Easy to Fix and Currently Are Not

The Cicero misattribution ("Tulius" for "Tullius"), the meta-tag misspelling of "Accomodation," and the published-copy typo "aray" for "array" are all quick corrections once someone is looking for them, and all currently sit in front of every visitor and every search engine.

## 11. Decide, Deliberately, What Belongs on the Reception Wall

The two framed portraits of Tanzanian public figures visible in the lobby photography are a small detail, but in a rebuilt brand environment, every visible element should be a deliberate choice rather than an inherited one — this is flagged as a decision point, not a recommendation either way.
