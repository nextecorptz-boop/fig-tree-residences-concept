# Fig Tree Residences — Storytelling Flow
### Phase 2: Experience Strategy — v1.0

This document describes how the experience should feel to move through, as a narrative arc rather than a set of pages — cinematic, not informational; a residence being discovered, not a hotel being explained. It describes emotional and structural rhythm only; no layout, animation code, or visual treatment is specified here (see `10_MOTION_STRATEGY.md` for the motion philosophy that will eventually support this flow).

## 1. Why "Cinematic" Is the Right Word, and What It Actually Means Here

Cinematic does not mean dramatic, fast-cut, or spectacular — none of that belongs anywhere near a brand built on the word "tranquil." It means the experience should have the qualities a well-made, slow film has: a deliberate opening shot that sets a mood before any dialogue happens, scenes that are allowed to breathe before the next one begins, and a sense that someone with intention chose exactly what to reveal and in what order. A hotel website explains itself in the first screen (name, star rating, book-now button). A cinematic residence experience opens on a single, held image — the property's own January 2025 drone shot is exactly this kind of frame — and lets a visitor arrive before it asks anything of them.

## 2. The Five-Act Shape of the Experience

**Act One — Arrival (Home).** A single, held establishing shot with almost nothing else on it: the drone photograph of the property in its setting, the fig-tree mark, and the Cicero line, allowed to sit in silence before any navigation, amenity list, or call to action competes for attention. The emotional job of this act is entirely to make someone feel they have just walked through a gate.

**Act Two — Discovery (Our Story, Location).** Once arrival has landed, the story opens outward: who this place is (the fig-tree metaphor, the quiet 2018 opening), and where it sits (200 metres from the Yacht Club, a garden peninsula rather than a strip of hotels). This act should feel like being walked somewhere by someone who knows it well, not like reading a company history.

**Act Three — Immersion (Rooms, Amenities, Gardens).** The visitor moves from being told about the place to being shown their place in it — a specific room, a specific garden bench, a specific rooftop sunset. This is the act where specificity (per `06_CONTENT_HIERARCHY.md`) matters more than anywhere else in the flow: generic amenity photography breaks the spell precisely at the moment a visitor is trying to picture themselves there.

**Act Four — Reassurance (Reviews, Security, Location detail).** Before ever asking for a decision, the experience should quietly answer the questions a careful guest is still holding — is this actually safe, has anyone else vouched for it, is it really as close to everything as it claims. This act should feel like a confidence being confirmed, not a sales objection being handled.

**Act Five — Invitation (Rates & Booking).** Only once arrival, discovery, immersion, and reassurance have been allowed to unfold does the experience ask for anything. The booking moment should feel like the natural, easy conclusion of everything that came before it — not a wall the visitor eventually has to climb to get what they came for. This is also where the single highest-risk gap identified in this project (no visible rates) must be closed for the entire cinematic arc to pay off; a beautiful four-act build-up followed by a missing fifth act is worse than no story at all.

## 3. What Never Interrupts the Story

Nothing in this flow should ever ask a visitor to make a decision, absorb a promotional interruption, or process an urgency device (countdown timers, "X people viewing this room," aggressive pop-ups) before the story has been allowed to land. Discovery found the current site's worst trust problems — the triplicated payment icons, the pre-filled fake success message — are exactly this kind of interruption, breaking narrative trust at the precise moments it matters most. The cinematic standard this flow sets is a direct rejection of that pattern.

## 4. How the Five Acts Map Back to the Guest Journey

Act One corresponds to the Homepage stage of `04_GUEST_JOURNEY.md`; Act Two to Discover Property and Location; Act Three to Explore Rooms, Amenities, and Gardens; Act Four to Reviews; Act Five to Booking. The journey document describes the emotional destination at each stage in isolation; this document's job is to insist those stages are experienced as a continuous, paced narrative rather than as seven disconnected pages a visitor happens to click through in any order.

## 5. The Test for Any Future Section

Any new page, module, or content block proposed in a later phase should be placed into one of these five acts before it is designed. If it does not clearly belong to Arrival, Discovery, Immersion, Reassurance, or Invitation, it is either redundant or it belongs in a sixth act (the post-departure moment named as an open gap in `04_GUEST_JOURNEY.md` and `07_INFORMATION_ARCHITECTURE.md`) that this project has not yet scoped.
