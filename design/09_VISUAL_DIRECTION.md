# Fig Tree Residences — Visual Direction
### Phase 3: Luxury Design Language — v2.0

This document is the photography and art-direction brief every future shoot, edit, or AI-generated image should be checked against. It directly answers the problems Phase 1's Photo Audit identified in the existing library and sets the standard for everything replacing it.

## 1. The Correction This Direction Exists to Make

Phase 1 found the newest and best-composed photographs in the entire project — the arched, palm-motif headboard bedrooms — undermined by a strong, uncorrected yellow-green colour cast. This is not a minor technical footnote; it is the starting brief for this entire document. Every future image must be graded toward the warm, golden, but *neutral-white* light already visible in the property's genuinely successful photography (the rooftop dusk shots, the garden breakfast tray) — warmth should come from the time of day and the light itself, never from an uncorrected white-balance error making walls look sickly.

## 2. Lighting Direction

Natural light only, and specifically the two moments the property's own existing photography already proves work best for it: soft, diffused midday light for interiors and gardens (avoiding harsh, direct overhead sun that flattens the mature garden greenery), and golden-hour/dusk light for the rooftop restaurant and any exterior shots meant to carry emotional weight. Artificial lighting, where unavoidable for interior shoots, should be colour-corrected to match daylight white balance (approximately 5000–5600K) rather than left at a warmer tungsten cast, which is the specific failure mode found in the current library.

## 3. Framing and Composition

Compositions should follow the "premium architectural magazine" reference directly: generous negative space around the subject, deliberate off-centre framing rather than a subject dead-centred in the frame, and a preference for a single, considered vantage point over multiple redundant angles of the same room — Phase 1 found at least three separate photographs of what appears to be the same living room, which is a symptom of composition-by-quantity rather than composition-by-intention. Every shot should be able to answer "what is this specifically showing that no other photograph in the library already shows" — if the answer is nothing, the shot is redundant.

## 4. Colour Grading Standard

All final images should be graded to sit within the palette defined in `03_DESIGN_TOKENS.md` — walls and neutral surfaces should read as true `cream.base`, not shifted toward green or yellow; the building's teal and the garden's terracotta should read as their true, saturated selves rather than muted or colour-cast. A simple technical check for any future image: a neutral white or cream wall in the frame should visually match the `cream.base` swatch when placed side by side — if it does not, the image has not been correctly graded and should not be approved for use.

## 5. Human Presence

Phase 1 found exactly one photograph of a person anywhere in the property's asset library, against an explicit written promise of "a happy, bright and friendly collection of professionals." Future photography must correct this directly: genuine, unposed-feeling staff moments (not stock-photo-style forced smiles to camera), and, where guest consent allows, authentic candid moments of people actually using the spaces — a guest reading on the garden bench, a family at the pool edge — rather than empty, people-less rooms throughout the entire library.

## 6. Material and Texture Direction

Photography should deliberately foreground the property's real materials rather than treating them as background: the teal ceramic and paint, the terracotta stonework, the rattan and timber furniture, the living green wall. Close, textural detail shots of these materials (a hand on a rattan armrest, morning light on the terracotta wall) are a valuable, currently entirely missing category and should be added specifically to give the Gardens and Amenities components in `04_COMPONENT_LIBRARY.md` something more tactile than wide establishing shots alone.

## 7. What to Never Shoot

No generic tropical-resort stock imagery unconnected to the actual property (a stand-in beach, a stock cocktail glass with no relation to the rooftop bar actually photographed) — everything in this library must be verifiably Fig Tree, not vaguely tropical. No wide-angle lens distortion exaggerating room size — Discovery's persona work in Phase 2 specifically flags the Business Traveler and Family personas needing to trust what a room actually looks like; a distorted wide shot that oversells space works directly against that trust. No harsh flash photography, and no photography styled to resemble a conventional OTA listing photo (evenly lit, centred, context-free) — every image should look like it could appear in an independent hospitality or design magazine feature, not a booking-engine thumbnail.

## 8. Priority Shot List Carried Forward From Discovery

Consistent with the gaps identified in Phase 1's Asset Inventory and Photo Audit, new photography should prioritise, in order: one genuine full-resolution hero/exterior image beyond the single existing drone shot; a distinct, correctly graded photograph for every room type the property actually sells; authentic human/staff presence; close material and texture detail shots; and a small set of night exterior photographs, none of which currently exist anywhere in the project.
