# Fig Tree Residences — Design Language
### Phase 3: Luxury Design Language — v2.0

This document defines the visual DNA of Fig Tree Residences: the underlying character every later token, component, and motion decision must express. It contains no mockups and no code. It exists so that any designer or engineer joining this project later can look at a finished screen and know, without being told, whether it belongs to this brand.

## 1. What This Language Is Built From

Every decision below is anchored to something Phase 1 discovery actually found, not invented from a mood board. The property already has a deep teal/turquoise-green independently present on its logo, its painted building, and its rooftop bar structure; a terracotta/coral independently present on its logo's fig fruit and its garden retaining walls; a fig-tree mark that draws roots with as much weight as canopy; and a printed brochure whose single best line — "the pursuit, even of the best things, ought to be calm and tranquil" — has never been used anywhere in its digital presence. This is a design language for finishing something that already exists, not inventing a new brand from nothing.

## 2. The Seven References, and What Each One Actually Means Here

**Tropical gardens.** Not decorative palm-leaf patterns dropped onto a template — the actual living wall, mature trees, and rattan garden furniture already photographed on the property. Green appears as a material found in nature (a wall of real leaves), not as a flat brand colour swatch.

**Quiet luxury.** The absence of ornament, not its accumulation. No gold trim, no heavy borders, no dense visual competition for attention. Luxury is communicated through restraint, correct proportion, and unhurried pacing — consistent with the Cicero line this brand already owns.

**Boutique serviced residences.** Small and specific, never scaled and generic. Every visual decision should read as "this exact place," never as a template that could be reskinned for any hospitality brand in the world.

**Ocean breeze.** Airiness and light movement, not literal wave graphics. Generous whitespace, soft gradients reminiscent of the Indian Ocean's colour range at different times of day, and a sense that air is moving through every layout rather than pressing down on it.

**Warm natural light.** Golden-hour warmth, not cool corporate blue-white lighting. The property's own strongest existing photography (the rooftop restaurant at dusk, the garden bench with morning light) already demonstrates this; the design language exists to make sure every future asset matches that same light quality rather than contradicting it with a cooler colour cast, which Phase 1's Photo Audit already flagged as a live problem.

**Editorial hospitality photography.** Full-bleed, considered, magazine-style images with generous breathing room around them — never cropped into small thumbnails competing with text and icons on a busy page.

**Premium architectural magazine.** Deliberate grid discipline, generous margins, and typography treated as an architectural element in its own right — the same visual confidence found in a well-art-directed spread in a magazine like Cereal or AD, not the dense, badge-covered layout of a typical OTA-facing hotel page.

## 3. The Explicit Anti-Reference: What This Must Never Look Like

A corporate hotel chain website is loud, dense, and interchangeable: stacked promotional banners, countdown-style urgency modules, star-rating badges, a wall of amenity icons competing for the same visual weight, and stock photography that could belong to any property on earth. Discovery's own Website Analysis found early symptoms of exactly this pattern already creeping into Fig Tree's current site — a triplicated payment icon, an unfinished FAQ accordion, generic "soft relaxing ocean colours" copy. Every principle in this design language is, in part, a direct correction of that drift back toward the calm, specific, editorial register this brand actually belongs in.

## 4. The Four Governing Principles

**Restraint is the luxury signal, not decoration.** Wherever a later decision must choose between adding an element and removing one, remove one, unless the addition is load-bearing content per Phase 2's content hierarchy.

**Materials over graphics.** Texture, colour, and pattern should read as coming from the real, physical property — its teal paint, its terracotta stone, its garden greenery, its wood and rattan furniture — rather than from abstract digital gradients or generic iconography.

**One held breath at a time.** Consistent with the five-act storytelling flow defined in Phase 2, no screen should try to say everything at once. Each section earns its own visual moment before the next begins.

**Specificity beats template.** Every visual choice should be traceable back to an actual fact about this actual property — its colours, its garden, its Yacht Club view — rather than a generic "tropical luxury" template that happens to have Fig Tree's logo dropped into it.

## 5. How the Rest of This Folder Builds on This

`02_DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` translates these principles into a working system; `03_DESIGN_TOKENS.md` gives that system concrete, engineer-usable values; `04_COMPONENT_LIBRARY.md` through `10_HIGGSFIELD_PREP.md` apply it to specific components, layout, motion, cursor, iconography, photography direction, and AI-video production briefs. Nothing in those documents should introduce a colour, a typeface, a motion pattern, or a component behaviour that cannot be justified against the five references and four principles set out above.
